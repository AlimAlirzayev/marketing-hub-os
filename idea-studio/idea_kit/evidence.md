# `evidence.md` — the effectiveness science (why emotion wins, with numbers)

The scientific spine under the whole studio: creative/emotional work is
not a taste preference, it is the **measured** path to profit. When a
stakeholder asks "why are we making art instead of a product slide",
this file is the answer. Every number carries its study.

---

## The core findings

- **Emotional beats rational on hard metrics.** "Emotional campaigns are
  almost twice as likely to result in top-box profit growth" — Binet &
  Field, *The Long and the Short of It* (IPA Databank, ~1,000
  effectiveness cases, 1980–2010).
- **The 60/40 rule.** Optimal budget balance ≈ **60% brand building
  (emotion-led, broad reach) / 40% activation**. Brands near this split
  outperformed on share growth, pricing power, profit margin and reduced
  price elasticity (Binet & Field, IPA/Thinkbox).
- **Fame campaigns win biggest.** Campaigns that get *talked about*
  "report the greatest profit growth… on all metrics including sales,
  share, price sensitivity, loyalty, and penetration" (IPA Databank).
- **Creativity is an efficiency multiplier — when run long.** Creatively
  awarded campaigns were **~12x as efficient** as non-awarded (1996–
  2008); the multiplier collapsed to **<4x** (2006–2018) as creativity
  got deployed in short-term bursts — Peter Field, *The Crisis in
  Creative Effectiveness* (IPA 2019).
- **Creative quality is the biggest controllable profit driver.** Strong
  creative delivers **36% higher profitability** than weak; the most
  creative+effective ads generate **4x+ profit** (Kantar, *The New Rules
  of Marketing Effectiveness*, 2022).
- **Measured emotion predicts growth.** System1's Star Rating + spend
  explains **up to 48% of brand growth** (250+ validations). The three
  metrics that matter: **Fame** (comes to mind), **Feeling** (positive
  emotion), **Fluency** (recognized fast from its assets).
- **Distinctiveness = pricing power.** Kantar BrandZ (40,000 brands):
  strong relationship between perceived uniqueness and willingness to
  pay more (Sharp/Ehrenberg-Bass: mental availability + distinctive
  assets drive growth).
- **ESOV is the growth driver.** Brands whose share of voice sits above
  their share of market tend to grow; below, they shrink (IPA Databank).
- **Effects compound with time.** "The total number of business effects
  rises steadily as the campaign length increases" — a platform run for
  years beats annual reinvention.

## Operational rules (how the studio applies this)

1. **Split the work:** brand-building and activation are separate
   workstreams with separate metrics (brand: fame/feeling/price
   elasticity; activation: conversion). One hybrid asset asked to do
   both under-delivers on both.
2. **Brief for fame** on brand work: talkability is the objective, not a
   bonus (Cadbury "Gorilla" carried zero product message).
3. **Gate on emotion, not recall:** judge brand assets by felt response
   (the rubric's Emotional-charge dimension), not message playback.
4. **Codify distinctive assets** and repeat them relentlessly — color,
   character, sound, line — so recognition becomes effortless.
5. **Commit to platforms for years;** refresh executions, don't replace
   the idea. Evaluate brand work on 6-month+ windows.
6. **Check pricing power:** did the work make people tolerate the price,
   not just click?

## Anti-patterns

```
✗ Judging all spend on in-quarter ROI (activation always wins the
  quarter and loses the decade — the 12x→4x collapse)
✗ Rational message-stuffing on brand work
✗ Campaign churn — resetting fame/fluency to zero every year
✗ Over-targeting: buying only high-intent narrow audiences when growth
  needs broad reach (penetration)
✗ Engagement-metric worship (clicks/likes are not validated predictors;
  emotion, fame, ESOV are)
✗ Cutting brand budget in downturns (negative ESOV = share loss)
```

## Measured proof campaigns

| Campaign | Measured result |
|---|---|
| **Cadbury "Gorilla"** (Fallon, 2007) | Sales ~+5% YoY in campaign month vs ~-7% trend earlier that year; IPA Effectiveness Grand Prix — https://ipa.co.uk/knowledge/case-studies/cadbury-s-dairy-milk-how-a-drumming-gorilla-beat-a-path-back-to-profitable-growth |
| **John Lewis Christmas** (adam&eveDDB) | 2012–15: +16% avg festive sales, >£8 profit per £1 (IPA Grand Prix 2016); decade case: £1.2bn incremental sales, £411m net profit — https://ipa.co.uk/knowledge/case-studies/john-lewis-an-amazing-decade |
| **Snickers "You're Not You…"** (BBDO, 2010–) | +15.9% global sales in year one; share up in 56/58 markets; ~$376m sales over two years — https://www.campaignlive.co.uk/article/case-study-fame-made-snickers-youre-not-when-youre-hungry-campaign-success/1410807 |
