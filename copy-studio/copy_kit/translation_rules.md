# Translation rules — AZ ↔ EN ↔ RU

Three working languages. Each has its own voice posture even though the
brand identity is one.

## Order of priority

1. **AZ** — primary. Always written first.
2. **EN** — cross-post / international LinkedIn. Adapted from AZ, not
   translated word-for-word.
3. **RU** — Russian-speaking AZ segment. Adapted from AZ.

**Never** machine-translate. Adapt. The headline that works in AZ rarely
works word-for-word in EN.

## AZ → EN adaptation rules

| AZ pattern | EN approach |
|---|---|
| Long compound sentence | Break into two shorter sentences. |
| "Siz" (formal you) | "You" (English has no formal distinction; tone carries via lexicon). |
| Verb at end of clause | Restructure to verb-second English position. |
| "Bizim üçün isə bu, fürsətdir" | "For us, that's an opportunity" — match the rhythm, not the literal words. |
| Idioms (e.g. "yola çıxmaq") | Use the English idiom that carries the same weight (e.g. "set off", "begin a journey"). |
| Cultural reference (Azerbaijani specific) | Make it geographic-explicit for international readers ("Baku-Tbilisi" not "Bakı-Tbilisi"). |

### Tone shift in EN

- Slightly more formal than AZ — AZ "calm authority" maps to EN
  "considered professionalism".
- No Americanisms. The EN voice is more Economist than Wired.
- Avoid contractions on serious copy ("you'll" → "you will" for headlines;
  contractions OK in conversational caption body).

## AZ → RU adaptation rules

| AZ pattern | RU approach |
|---|---|
| "Siz" form | "Вы" (formal) throughout. Never "ты". |
| Headline brevity | Russian tends ~30% longer than AZ; cut harder. |
| Hashtags | Mix of Cyrillic + Latin (#ХалкСигорта + #XalqSigorta). |
| Cultural references | Keep Azerbaijan-specific markers (Bakı-Tbilisi reads better than "Baku-Tbilisi" for RU-AZ audience). |

### Tone shift in RU

- The "calm authority" maps to RU "сдержанный профессионализм" — calm, no
  exclamations, formal "вы" throughout.
- No Russian internet slang ever.
- Use proper punctuation: «christmas-tree quotes», not "straight ones".

## When the languages disagree

Sometimes a hook that's strong in AZ won't have an EN equivalent. The
rule: **don't force the EN to follow AZ if it breaks the EN voice.**
Better to write a different EN headline that serves the same product
truth than to translate badly.

Example:
- AZ: "Yeni reys başladı." (4 words, declarative, perfect rhythm)
- EN literal: "A new route has started." (clunky)
- EN adapted: "The Baku–Tbilisi train is departing." (different words, same energy)

The brand voice is the same; the words are different. That's correct.
