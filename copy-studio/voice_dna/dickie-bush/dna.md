# Voice DNA — `dickie-bush`

**Atomic essays.** One idea, ~250 words, shipped daily. The voice for
compressed, self-contained micro-content: a single insight argued
completely in the space of a screenshot. Shares a system with
`nicolas-cole` (they built Ship 30 for 30 together) — Cole is the
long-form architecture; Bush is the atomic unit. Use this voice when the
deliverable is a short, complete, publishable thought. Brand-agnostic.

## Lineage

- **Dickie Bush** — Ship 30 for 30 co-founder; grew 50k+ audience in
  under a year by shipping daily.
- The atomic-essay format: "longer than a tweet, shorter than a blog
  post" — one idea per essay, published as a visual unit.
- The build-in-public / daily-shipping tradition: volume + feedback over
  perfectionism.

## Voice fingerprint

- **One idea per essay. Complete.** Not a teaser, not a thread of loose
  thoughts — a single point, fully landed, in ~250 words.
- **Ship over polish.** Momentum beats perfection; the habit is the
  strategy. Publish, learn, repeat.
- **The hook is a separate asset.** The lead-in line (originally the
  lead tweet) sells the essay; it's written to stop the scroll, then the
  essay pays it off.
- **4A idea generation.** The same topic re-approached through
  actionable, analytical, aspirational, anthropological lenses — one
  topic becomes many essays.
- **Tight compression.** Every sentence carries weight; 250 words leaves
  no room for filler.
- **Data-driven iteration.** What resonates gets expanded; what flops
  teaches.

## Hook structure (the lead-in line)

1. **The bold claim** — "Perfectionism is just fear wearing a suit."
2. **The specific promise** — "The 250-word habit that changed how I
   think."
3. **The relatable pain** — "You don't have a writing problem. You have
   a shipping problem."

## Body structure (the atomic essay)

- **Open restating the hook's idea in one sharp line.**
- **Argue it in 2–3 tight moves** — an example, a reason, a reframe.
- **Close with the single takeaway** — often a one-line aphorism the
  reader can carry away.
- Total: ~200–280 words. If it needs more, it's two essays.

## Punctuation discipline

- **Short sentences, periods.** Line breaks generously.
- **Lists** when the argument has parts.
- **Bold** for the one load-bearing phrase (platform permitting).
- **Exclamation marks** — rare.

## Hashtag posture

Minimal (0–2). The hook does the work.

## Output structure

```
[Hook — one line, written to stop the scroll.]

[Restate the idea, sharp, one line.]

[Move 1 — example or reason.]
[Move 2 — reframe or consequence.]

[The takeaway — one carryable line.]
```

## Anti-patterns

```
✗ Two ideas crammed into one essay
✗ A teaser that doesn't fully land its point
✗ Filler sentences to reach length (compression is the style)
✗ Perfectionism that delays shipping
✗ A hook the essay doesn't pay off
```

## Example — universal (atomic essay, brand-neutral)

```
You don't have a writing problem. You have a shipping problem.

Every draft you sit on teaches you nothing.

The draft you publish teaches you everything: what lands, what confuses,
what people quote back to you. One day of feedback beats a month of
private editing.

The writers you admire aren't better. They're better *informed* —
because they ship daily and read the data.

Stop polishing. Start shipping. Volume is how taste gets built.
```

## Pairs with visual style

`editorial-documentary` or `financial-restraint` — the essay itself is
the visual unit; keep the frame plain.

## When NOT to use this voice

- Full articles / newsletters (use `nicolas-cole`).
- Narrative micro-essays with a story arc (use `eddie-shleyner`).
- Anything requiring nuance beyond one idea (wrong container).
