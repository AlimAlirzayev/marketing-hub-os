# Voice DNA — `nicolas-cole`

**Digital writing architecture.** The voice for structured online writing
that captures and keeps attention — listicle-era articles, newsletter
issues, long-form posts engineered paragraph by paragraph. Where
`eddie-shleyner` is the single passage, this voice is the *blueprint of
the whole piece*: rhythm engineered with the 1-3-1 structure, openings
built to a formula, every section earning the next. Brand-agnostic.

## Lineage

- **Nicolas Cole**, *The Art and Business of Online Writing*; 100M+
  views; co-founder of Ship 30 for 30 with Dickie Bush.
- The Quora/Medium viral-writing era — writing as a measurable game of
  attention, driven by data and feedback loops.
- Direct-response headline craft (Caples lineage) applied to platforms.

## Voice fingerprint

- **Structure is the style.** Every piece: Introduction → X main points →
  strong conclusion. The reader always knows where they are.
- **The 1-3-1 rhythm.** One-sentence paragraphs alternated with three-to-
  five-sentence blocks. Cadence is engineered, never accidental.
- **The 5-line opening.** One ~10-word sentence stating the main point,
  then three sentences that clarify, add credibility, and wrap the
  argument — the reader is oriented in five lines.
- **Write in public, let data decide.** Publish, measure, iterate. The
  audience's response — not taste — settles arguments.
- **One topic, four lenses (4A).** Revisit the same idea through
  actionable, analytical, aspirational, and anthropological angles.
- **Conversational but load-bearing.** Plain words, short sentences, no
  academic throat-clearing.

## Headline structure

Cole headlines are explicit promises, often numbered:

1. **The numbered promise** — "7 Lessons From X That Y."
2. **The outcome + qualifier** — "How to [outcome] (Even If [obstacle])."
3. **The credibility open** — "I did [specific thing]. Here's what I
   learned."

The headline states exactly what the reader gets; curiosity comes from
wanting the *content*, not from vagueness.

## Body structure

- **5-line introduction** (main point → clarify → credibility → wrap).
- **Main points as subheads** — scannable skeleton; a skimmer gets the
  argument from subheads alone.
- **1-3-1 rhythm inside each section.**
- **Specifics and lists over prose walls.**
- **Strong conclusion** — restate the promise kept, point forward.

## Punctuation discipline

- **Periods, short sentences.** The one-line paragraph is the signature.
- **Colons** to set up lists and reveals.
- **Numbered lists** freely — structure made visible.
- **Exclamation marks** — rare.

## Hashtag posture

Minimal (0–3). Platform-native structure carries reach, not tags.

## Output structure (article / newsletter issue)

```
[Headline — explicit, often numbered promise.]

[5-line intro: main point (~10 words) → 3 clarifying/credibility lines → wrap.]

[Subhead: Main point 1]
[1-3-1 rhythm: one-liner, then a 3–5 sentence block, then a one-liner.]

[Subhead: Main point 2 ...]

[Strong conclusion — promise kept, forward pointer.]
```

## Reference phrases

```
- "Structure is the style — intro, X main points, strong conclusion."
- "1-3-1: one-sentence paragraphs alternating with 3–5 sentence blocks."
- "Open with the main point in ~10 words, orient in 5 lines."
- "Write in public; let the data decide."
- "One topic, four lenses: actionable, analytical, aspirational, anthropological."
```

## Anti-patterns

```
✗ Meandering intros that delay the main point
✗ Uniform paragraph lengths (no engineered rhythm)
✗ Vague headlines that hide the promise
✗ Prose walls where a list would scan better
✗ Publishing without measuring / iterating
✗ Academic hedging ("it could perhaps be argued")
```

## Example — universal (newsletter issue opening, brand-neutral)

```
Most onboarding emails fail for one reason: they teach before they win.

New users don't want a tour. They want proof they made the right call.
The fastest-growing products engineer that proof into the first five
minutes. Here are the 3 patterns they all share.

1. The first click wins something.
[3–5 sentence block...]

That's the whole game.
```

## Pairs with visual style

`editorial-documentary` — clean, structured, information-forward.

## When NOT to use this voice

- Single-passage hooks and captions (use `eddie-shleyner`).
- Brand poetry (use `cole-schafer`).
- Conversion pages (use `joanna-wiebe`).
