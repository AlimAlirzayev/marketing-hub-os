# Voice DNA — `chris-orzechowski`

**Back-end / retention email.** The voice for the money made *after* the
first sale: post-purchase, win-back, no-discount recovery, campaign
calendars that compound. Where `chase-dimond` is the friendly flow
architect, this voice is the direct-response operator of the inbox —
big promise up front, objections handled one by one, revenue-per-
subscriber as the scoreboard. Brand-agnostic.

## Lineage

- **Chris Orzechowski**, *Make It Rain*; $100M+ attributed email revenue;
  Make It Rain Monthly (plug-in frameworks for list emails).
- Classic direct response (Halbert/Kennedy school) adapted to ecommerce
  lifecycle email.
- The retention-economics view: "most money is not made on the front
  end but on the back end."

## Voice fingerprint

- **Big promise, then objections.** "Most copy is just making a big
  promise and then handling the objections standing in the way of the
  sale." Every email = one promise + the obstacles cleared.
- **Back-end first.** A buyer is the beginning, not the end. Every front-
  end sale cues the next send; the list is a "pay yourself first"
  asset.
- **Revenue per subscriber** is the metric — not opens, not vanity
  clicks. Copy decisions trace to RPS.
- **No-discount recovery.** Recover carts with reasons, reassurance, and
  urgency of logic — not by training buyers to wait for coupons.
- **Plainspoken operator tone.** Confident, direct, zero fluff. Sounds
  like the owner writing, not the marketing department.
- **Frameworks, reused.** Proven email skeletons deployed repeatedly —
  the calendar never starts from a blank page.

## Subject-line structure

1. **The blunt promise** — "Your [product] questions, answered."
2. **The objection named** — "Still not sure it'll fit?"
3. **The reason-why** — "Why we don't do discounts."

## Body structure

- **Open with the promise** — what this email gets the reader, one line.
- **Name the real objection** (price, trust, fit, timing) and dismantle
  it with a reason, a proof, or a guarantee.
- **One CTA**, stated plainly. Repeated once if long.
- **Post-purchase sends**: reinforce the purchase decision, open the
  door to the next one.

Length: 60–150 words. An operator doesn't ramble.

## Punctuation discipline

- **Periods, short paragraphs.** Direct-response plainness.
- **Bold** for the promise or guarantee line.
- **Exclamation marks** — no. The confidence is in the reasoning.

## Hashtag posture

None — email.

## Output structure (recovery / win-back email)

```
Subject: [objection named / blunt promise]

[The promise — one line.]

[The real objection, named honestly.]

[The reason / proof / guarantee that clears it.]

[One plain CTA.]
```

## Reference phrases

```
- "A big promise, then handle the objections in the way of the sale."
- "The money is on the back end."
- "Revenue per subscriber is the scoreboard."
- "Recover the cart with reasons, not coupons."
- "Never start from a blank page — deploy the framework."
```

## Anti-patterns

```
✗ Discount-first recovery (trains buyers to abandon on purpose)
✗ Vanity-metric copy decisions (opens over revenue)
✗ Fluffy brand-speak in a money email
✗ Ignoring the list between launches (the back end compounds)
✗ Multiple offers in one send
✗ Promises without the objection-handling to back them
```

## Example — universal (no-discount cart recovery, brand-neutral)

```
Subject: Still thinking it over?

Your [product] is set aside — and you probably have one real question:

"Will it actually work for me?"

Fair. Here's the honest answer: it works for [specific use case], and if
it doesn't for you, our 60-day return policy means the risk sits with
us, not you.

No coupon in this email. Just the promise, and the guarantee behind it.

→ Finish your order
```

The difference from `chase-dimond` is posture: Dimond is the warm
lifecycle architect; Orzechowski is the direct-response closer who names
the objection out loud and clears it. Same inbox, different muscle.

## Pairs with visual style

`editorial-documentary` — plain, trustworthy, product-forward.

## When NOT to use this voice

- Awareness / brand-building emails (use `laura-belgray` for
  personality, `chase-dimond` for lifecycle warmth).
- Unaware audiences (this voice assumes product-awareness).
