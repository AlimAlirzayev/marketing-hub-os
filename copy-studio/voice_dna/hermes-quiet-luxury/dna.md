# Voice DNA — `hermes-quiet-luxury`

Sensory, observational, slow. Copy that reads like it was written by hand
in a notebook. For Xalq Sigorta heritage moments, premium product
introductions, and the rare instances where the brand wants to feel
crafted, not sold.

## Lineage

- **Hermès catalog copy**, especially the silk scarf and Petit h
  series — the "object as artifact" voice.
- **Bottega Veneta** editorial captions — the post-Daniel-Lee restraint.
- **Aesop product descriptions** — the apothecary-poetry voice.
- **Patek Philippe** body copy — the "you never actually own" tradition.
- **The New Yorker fiction openings** — the sentence-as-precise-instrument
  school.

## Voice fingerprint

- **Sensory verbs.** "Folded just so." "Settles into the seam." "Catches
  the light." The reader is given something to imagine seeing or feeling.
- **Observation, not assertion.** The voice describes; it does not promise.
- **Quiet rhythm.** Sentences vary in length but each one settles. No
  driving energy. No urgency.
- **Verbs over adjectives.** "The silk holds the weight" beats "the
  beautiful silk."
- **Long-tail relationships.** The brand has been somewhere for a while
  and will be there a while longer.
- **No call-to-action energy.** The CTA, when it appears, is matter-of-
  fact. The reader knows what to do.

## Headline structure

Hermès headlines are one of these:

1. **The Observation**
   - "Three drops on porcelain."
   - "Bir kart üçün hazır."
   - Format: [specific image] + [understated framing]

2. **The Quiet Verb**
   - "Bir yola." (To a journey.)
   - "Folded once."

3. **The Almost-Sentence**
   - "On the train, before light."
   - "Bakıdan çıxır."

Length: 3-7 words. Period as soft punctuation. Often ambiguous —
the sentence completes only when you finish the body.

## Sub-headline structure

The sub continues the headline as if both were one breath.

- "Sığorta, yola çıxmadan əvvəl." (Insurance, before setting off.)
- "A document. A journey. A morning."

## Body copy structure

- **Open with a sense detail.** Light, sound, texture, time of day.
- **Stay in the moment.** The body lives inside the world the headline
  set up. No leaving for "let me tell you about our product."
- **The product appears almost incidentally.** Sometimes as a noun in a
  longer sentence about the customer's day.
- **Closing is restated quiet.** No CTA fanfare.

Average length: 60-100 words. Reads slowly. The reader feels the pause.

## Punctuation discipline

- **Periods.** Frequent. Each sentence is a beat.
- **Em-dashes** allowed for the side observation — like this — but not
  for emphasis.
- **Semicolons** allowed sparingly; the rhythm sometimes asks for them.
- **Commas** for music, not just grammar.
- **NO** exclamation marks. **NO** ellipses. **NO** question marks
  except in rare rhetorical settings.

## Hashtag posture

- 0-3 hashtags maximum. Often zero.
- If used: never the brand name first (too on-the-nose for this voice).
  Lead with the topic or place.
- No emoji, no special characters.

## Caption structure

```
[Headline — observational fragment, period.]

[Body — 2-3 paragraphs of sensory observation. The product is a noun
in the world, not the subject of the sentence.]

[Closing — quiet, restated. No CTA fanfare.]

[Light hashtags — topic / region, not brand-first.]
```

## Reference phrases

```
- "Hermès / Bottega / Aesop product-poetry tradition — sensory,
   observational, slow."
- "Verbs over adjectives. Observation, not assertion."
- "The product appears as a noun in the customer's day, not as the
   subject of the sentence."
- "Long-tail brand presence — has been here, will be here."
- "No CTA energy. The customer knows."
```

## Anti-patterns

```
✗ Adjective stacking
✗ "Discover", "Imagine", "Experience" — too active
✗ Urgency words ("now", "today", "limited")
✗ Exclamation marks of any kind
✗ Multiple hashtags
✗ Emoji
✗ Feature lists
✗ Brand-first sentence openers
✗ "We at Xalq Sigorta..." — voice fails immediately
✗ Sentence fragments used for emphasis (the voice's fragments are
   intentional, not punchline-style)
```

## Example — Xalq Sigorta travel insurance, Hermès voice

### Headline options

```
1. Bakıdan çıxır.
2. Bir səhər, bir yol.
3. Pasport, bilet, sığorta — sıraylə.
```

### Caption (full Hermès structure, AZ)

```
Bakıdan çıxır.

Qatar saat 21:40-da yola düşür. Kupedə yastı işıq.
Pəncərədə Qaradağ keçidi, sonra Şirvan, sonra yox olur.
Səhər tezdən — Tbilisi.

Pasport, bilet, sığorta. Üçü üçün də vaxt var,
amma sığorta üçün vaxt həmişə bir az əvvəldir.

xalqsigorta.az

#BakıTbilisi  #SəyahətSığortası
```

The difference from the other voices: the train ride is the subject of
the copy. The insurance is one of three nouns in a list. The CTA is one
domain name on its own line — no urgency, no number, no plea.

This voice reads slowly because it is slow. It treats the reader as
someone with time to spare.

## When NOT to use this voice

- Topical / deadline-driven campaigns (the voice has no urgency
  vocabulary — use `halbert-direct-response` instead)
- Product feature comparisons (the voice doesn't list)
- B2B / serious announcements (use `financial-restraint-az`)
- Anything where the CTA matters (the voice intentionally soft-pedals
  the ask)

This voice is for moments where Xalq Sigorta wants to be felt, not heard.
Reserve it for 1-3 campaigns a year — using it weekly would dilute its
power and start to read as affected.
