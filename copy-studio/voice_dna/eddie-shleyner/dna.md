# Voice DNA — `eddie-shleyner`

Short-form **craft** copy: hooks, micro-essays, captions, ad copy, and any
piece whose job is to be read all the way through. The voice that teaches
with a story and keeps you on the slippery slide — one idea per sentence,
each line pulling you to the next. Clear, concise, vivid, emotional.
Brand-agnostic.

## Lineage

- **Eddie Shleyner**, VeryGoodCopy — micro-lessons; one persuasion
  principle at a time; *Very Good Copy: 207 Micro-Lessons*.
- **Joe Sugarman**, *The Adweek Copywriting Handbook* — the "slippery
  slide" and "seeds of curiosity"; the first sentence's only job is the
  second.
- The micro-essay tradition: a single idea, fully landed, in under a
  minute of reading.

## Voice fingerprint

- **One idea per sentence.** Short. Punchy. Each line a single step.
- **Bucket brigades.** Every few lines, a tiny conversational hinge —
  "Here's the thing." "But it gets worse." "So what happened?" — to pull
  the reader forward.
- **Open with a hook that creates a gap.** A surprising fact, a tension,
  a half-told story the reader must finish.
- **Teach through story.** A small, specific anecdote carries the lesson;
  the point lands at the end, earned.
- **Vivid and concrete.** Show, don't summarize. Sensory detail over
  abstraction.
- **Emotion is the cornerstone.** Clear + concise gets read; *emotional*
  gets remembered and shared.
- **White space.** Lots of line breaks. Built for the scroll.

## Headline / hook structure

1. **The curiosity gap** — "I almost deleted the best line I ever wrote."
2. **The counter-intuitive claim** — "Good copy isn't written. It's
   assembled."
3. **The micro-story open** — drop the reader into a specific scene mid-
   moment.

The hook's only job: earn the second line. Nothing else.

## Body structure (micro-essay / caption)

- **Hook** — one line, creates the gap.
- **Setup** — 2–4 short lines, build the small story or tension.
- **Bucket brigade** — a one-line hinge to keep momentum.
- **Turn** — the insight, the surprise, the lesson revealed.
- **Land** — the single takeaway, stated plainly. Often the shortest line.

Length: short. A micro-essay is 80–200 words. Every line removable
without loss should be removed.

## Punctuation discipline

- **Periods.** Many of them. Short sentences are the whole style.
- **Line breaks** as pacing — one idea per line, often one line per
  paragraph.
- **Em-dashes** and **colons** for the reveal.
- **Question marks** for the bucket-brigade hinges.
- **Exclamation marks** — rare; the energy is in the rhythm.

## Hashtag posture

Light (≤3) on social. The hook carries attention, not the tags.

## Output structure (micro-essay)

```
[Hook — one line, opens a curiosity gap.]

[Setup line.]
[Setup line.]

[Bucket brigade — "Here's the thing:"]

[Turn — the insight or surprise.]

[Land — the single takeaway, shortest line.]
```

## Reference phrases

```
- "One idea per sentence."
- "Every sentence's only job is to get the next one read."
- "Bucket brigades keep them on the slippery slide."
- "Teach through a small, specific story."
- "Clear and concise gets read; emotional gets remembered."
- "If a line can be removed without loss, remove it."
```

## Anti-patterns

```
✗ Two or three ideas crammed into one sentence
✗ A hook that summarizes instead of opening a gap
✗ Abstraction where a concrete image would land harder
✗ Walls of text with no line breaks (death on the scroll)
✗ A lesson stated up front instead of earned at the end
✗ Filler lines that don't move the reader to the next one
✗ Corporate flatness — no tension, no emotion
```

## Example — universal (LinkedIn micro-essay, brand-neutral)

```
I almost cut the best sentence I ever wrote.

It was too short. Too plain. It didn't sound like "copywriting."

Here's the thing:

The client read the draft and stopped on that exact line.
"That's it," she said. "That's the whole thing."

The fancy sentences impressed me.
The plain one moved her.

Write to be understood, not to be admired.
```

The difference from `joanna-wiebe` is altitude: Wiebe engineers a page to
convert; Shleyner engineers a single passage to be *finished*. Use this
voice for the hook, the caption, the line that has to survive the scroll.

## Pairs with visual style

`editorial-documentary` or `financial-restraint` — plain visuals let the
words carry. Avoid ornament that competes with the line.

## When NOT to use this voice

- Full landing / sales pages (use `joanna-wiebe`).
- Email flows (use `chase-dimond`).
- Brand-prestige poetry (use `cole-schafer`).
