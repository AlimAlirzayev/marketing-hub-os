# Voice DNA — `chris-do`

**Consultative brand narrative.** The voice for B2B and service-brand
copy that sells expertise without selling — case-study narratives,
service pages, proposals, brand-strategy decks, thought-leadership that
positions premium. Built on story ("a product without a story is a
commodity") and Socratic restraint (questions over pitches). Use when
the offer is expertise and the buyer must *conclude on their own* that
you're the answer. Brand-agnostic.

## Lineage

- **Chris Do**, The Futur — Emmy-winning designer; "sell without
  selling"; the Socratic Six; brevity as a discipline; clients from Sony
  to Nike.
- The brand-strategy tradition: story as the price-premium mechanism.
- The consultative-sales school: listen, ask, reframe — never push.

## Voice fingerprint

- **Story makes the premium.** "A product without a story is a
  commodity. A product with a story is a brand." Every piece carries a
  narrative, not a feature sheet.
- **Ask, don't pitch.** Questions do the persuading. The copy poses the
  question the buyer is already privately asking, then lets the answer
  emerge.
- **Raise objections first.** Name the buyer's doubt before they do —
  it converts skepticism into trust.
- **Brevity is respect.** Say exactly what needs saying, stop. Economy
  of words signals seniority.
- **Teach to sell.** Generosity with real expertise is the sales
  strategy; the reader should leave smarter even if they never buy.
- **Human stakes in every story.** The client's fear, the turning point,
  the transformation — the Hero's Journey with the *customer* as hero,
  the brand as guide.

## Headline structure

1. **The reframing question** — "What is a logo actually worth?"
2. **The quiet authority claim** — "We don't design logos. We build the
   reason people pay more."
3. **The transformation** — "From [before-state] to [after-state]:
   how [client] did it."

## Body structure

- **Open with the client's situation** — the doubt, the stakes, in
  human terms. The customer is the hero; the brand is the guide.
- **The turning point** — the question or insight that changed the
  frame (not the deliverable list).
- **The transformation, with proof** — specific outcomes, numbers where
  they exist.
- **Raise the objection yourself** — "You might be thinking this only
  works for big budgets. Here's why it doesn't."
- **Close with an invitation, not a pitch** — a question or a low-
  pressure next step ("Worth a conversation?").

## Punctuation discipline

- **Short declaratives and honest questions.** Both carry the style.
- **Periods** as confidence. **Question marks** as the sales engine.
- **Exclamation marks** — no. Authority doesn't exclaim.

## Hashtag posture

Minimal (0–3) — this voice lives on service pages, decks, and LinkedIn
thought-leadership; the ideas carry the reach.

## Output structure (case narrative / service page)

```
[Reframing question or quiet authority claim.]

[The client's situation — human stakes, the doubt. 2–3 lines.]

[The turning point — the insight that changed the frame.]

[The transformation — specific proof.]

[The objection, raised by us first, answered honestly.]

[Invitation — a question, a low-pressure next step.]
```

## Reference phrases

```
- "A product without a story is a commodity."
- "Never sell. Seek to understand. Raise objections first."
- "Questions do the persuading."
- "Brevity is respect — say it, then stop."
- "The customer is the hero; the brand is the guide."
```

## Anti-patterns

```
✗ Feature/deliverable lists in place of a narrative
✗ High-pressure closes ("Book now — slots filling fast!")
✗ The brand as the hero of its own case study
✗ Hiding from objections instead of raising them
✗ Jargon-dense strategy-speak ("synergistic brand activations")
✗ Long-windedness — seniority is economy
```

## Example — universal (service-brand case narrative, brand-neutral)

```
What's a rebrand actually worth?

When [client] came to us, their product was better than their biggest
competitor's — and priced 30% lower. Nobody could tell. The market read
"cheaper" as "worse."

The turning point wasn't a new logo. It was one question: what do your
best customers say the product changed for them?

Their answers became the brand. Same product, new story — and within a
year, price parity with the market leader, no lost volume.

You might be thinking a story can't move price. That's what they
thought, too.

Worth a conversation?
```

## Pairs with visual style

`financial-restraint` or `editorial-documentary` — considered, senior,
whitespace as confidence.

## When NOT to use this voice

- Fast conversion offers (use `joanna-wiebe` / `halbert-direct-response`).
- B2C impulse products (use `chase-dimond` / `cole-schafer`).
- Feed-native personal content (use `justin-welsh` / `jasmin-alic`).
