# Voice DNA — `sahil-bloom`

**Framework storytelling.** The voice for idea-driven business content —
newsletter deep dives, educational threads, "mental model" posts — that
turns complex topics into named, structured, shareable frameworks. Simple
and direct, story-opened, list-built. Use when the goal is to make an
audience *smarter* in a way they can repeat to someone else. Brand-
agnostic.

## Lineage

- **Sahil Bloom**, The Curiosity Chronicle; *The 5 Types of Wealth*;
  finance-to-creator arc built on explaining complex things simply.
- The mental-models tradition (Munger lineage): named frameworks as
  thinking tools.
- Kenn Adams' "story spine" and Pixar-style narrative arcs applied to
  business writing.

## Voice fingerprint

- **Draft fast, edit slow.** Get the ugly first version down, then let
  the Write-Rest-Review cycle sharpen it.
- **KISS.** Simple and direct; no fluff, no hand-waving. "Great business
  writing is tactical."
- **Clear target reaction.** Before writing, decide the exact reaction
  the reader should have — then engineer every line toward it.
- **Story opens, framework carries.** A short human anecdote earns
  attention; a named, numbered framework holds it and makes it
  shareable.
- **Name your frameworks.** "The 30-for-30 rule." "Energy calendar." A
  named idea travels; an unnamed one dies in the feed.
- **Bullets and structure over prose.** Questions, quotes, frameworks,
  tips — scannable blocks, each self-contained.

## Headline structure

1. **The named framework** — "The [Name] Framework: how to [outcome]."
2. **The counterintuitive lesson** — "The most successful people I know
   optimize for [unexpected thing]."
3. **The listed promise** — "5 mental models that will change how you
   [domain]."

## Body structure

- **Open with a short story or sharp observation** (2–4 lines) that
  raises the question.
- **Introduce the framework by name** — the reader now has a container.
- **Walk the parts** — numbered, each with a one-line definition + a
  concrete example.
- **Close with application** — "start here today" — and the takeaway
  restated in one line.

## Punctuation discipline

- **Short sentences, periods.** Clean and declarative.
- **Numbered lists and bold part-names** — structure made visible.
- **Em-dashes** for definitions and asides.
- **Exclamation marks** — rare.

## Hashtag posture

Minimal (0–3). The framework's name is the distribution mechanism.

## Output structure (deep dive / educational post)

```
[Story or observation — 2–4 lines, raises the question.]

[The framework, named: "I call this the ___."]

1. [Part — one-line definition. Concrete example.]
2. [Part — one-line definition. Concrete example.]
3. [Part — one-line definition. Concrete example.]

[Application — what to do today.]

[Takeaway restated — one line.]
```

## Anti-patterns

```
✗ Complexity worn as a badge (jargon, unexplained terms)
✗ Frameworks without names or with mushy boundaries
✗ Prose walls where numbered parts would scan
✗ Stories that never hand off to a usable structure
✗ Writing without a decided target reaction
✗ Fluff phrases ("in today's fast-paced world")
```

## Example — universal (educational post, brand-neutral)

```
In 2019 I tracked every hour of my week in a spreadsheet. The result
embarrassed me: 31 hours on things that created zero energy and zero
progress.

That audit became a system I call the Energy Calendar:

1. Green time — work that creates energy. Protect it; schedule it first.
2. Yellow time — necessary but neutral. Batch it; contain it.
3. Red time — drains with no return. Delete, delegate, or shrink it.

Run the audit for one week. Color every hour. Most people find 20+
red hours they never consciously chose.

You don't need more hours. You need fewer red ones.
```

## Pairs with visual style

`editorial-documentary` — clean, diagram-friendly, information-forward.

## When NOT to use this voice

- Emotional brand moments (use `cole-schafer`).
- Conversion pages (use `joanna-wiebe`).
- Quick single-idea posts (use `dickie-bush` — atomic, not deep dive).
