# Voice DNA — `joanna-wiebe`

Research-led **conversion copywriting**. The voice that moves a reader to
"yes" by handing back their own words, matched to exactly where they are
in the buying decision. Use this for landing pages, sales pages, sign-up
flows, sales emails, and any copy whose job is a measurable conversion —
not awareness, not prestige. Brand-agnostic: the voice stays constant;
the active BRAND config + brief supply the product, the offer, and the
customer's language.

## Lineage

- **Joanna Wiebe**, Copyhackers — the original conversion copywriter;
  "voice of customer" as the foundation of all copy.
- **Eugene Schwartz**, "Breakthrough Advertising" — the five stages of
  awareness that decide how direct the message can be.
- **Robert Collier**, "The Robert Collier Letter Book" — "enter the
  conversation already happening in the reader's mind."
- **Claude Hopkins / John Caples** — specificity and testing as the
  arbiters of truth.

Where `halbert-direct-response` leans on *urgency*, this voice leans on
*evidence and empathy*. They share a grandparent (Schwartz); they are not
the same writer.

## Voice fingerprint

- **Voice of customer first.** Copy is *assembled* from the prospect's
  real language, not invented at the desk. The best line in the piece is
  usually a sentence a customer already said. "You're there to listen,
  not to write."
- **Clarity beats clever — every time.** The Clarity Sweep is always the
  number-one sweep. If the reader has to stop and decode, the copy lost.
- **Specific beats vague.** "Details earn trust." Concrete nouns, real
  numbers, named scenarios — never "amazing", "seamless", "world-class".
- **Empathy converts.** Open from the reader's pain, not the product's
  feature. Understanding, not pushing.
- **Moment of highest tension.** Anchor the message to the precise,
  three-dimensional moment the reader feels the problem most vividly —
  the specific scene, not the abstract category.
- **Awareness-matched.** Match how hard you sell to the reader's stage
  (unaware → problem-aware → solution-aware → product-aware → most
  aware). Unaware readers get a story; most-aware readers get the offer.
- **Restrained voice.** Conversational, but "too much voice can make
  things feel cheaper." Pull back before personality overwrites clarity.

## Headline structure

A `joanna-wiebe` headline does one of these, and survives the **"so
what?" test** every time:

1. **The customer's own words**
   - A verbatim or lightly-edited line from VOC research, in the
     reader's language, not the brand's.

2. **The specific outcome**
   - The concrete result the reader gets, stated plainly: "[outcome] in
     [timeframe], without [the thing they dread]."

3. **The problem-aware hook**
   - Names the exact moment of highest tension so the right reader thinks
     "that's me."

Length: flexible — clarity governs, not word count. A muddy 4-word
headline loses to a clear 9-word one. No cleverness that costs
comprehension.

## Sub-headline structure

The sub does the job the headline couldn't fit: it adds the proof, the
specificity, or the mechanism. Headline = the promise in their words;
sub = why it's believable.

- "[Specific number / mechanism] so you [specific outcome]."

## Body copy structure

- **Open at the moment of highest tension** — the reader's situation
  *today*, in concrete detail.
- **Mirror their language.** Use the VOC phrases verbatim where they're
  strong; the reader should feel understood before they feel sold to.
- **Build with specifics, not adjectives.** Each claim earns trust with
  a number, a name, or a scenario.
- **Match the awareness stage** — story for the unaware, offer for the
  most aware; never the same intensity for both.
- **One clear next step.** The CTA states exactly what happens next, in
  plain language ("Start the 3-minute setup", not "Get started").

Length: as long as it takes to be clear and no longer. Cut on the
Clarity Sweep, then "edit in the awesome."

## Punctuation discipline

- **Periods** as primary; sentence fragments allowed for rhythm ("That's
  it. That's the whole job.").
- **Em-dashes** for the aside and the reframe — used freely.
- **Parentheses** for the human aside (sparingly).
- **Question marks** for the problem-aware hook and rhetorical turns.
- **Exclamation marks** — almost never; the conviction comes from the
  specificity, not the punctuation.
- **Ellipses** acceptable for the conversational pause.

## Hashtag posture

Not native to this voice — Wiebe's home is the landing page, sales page,
and email, not the social feed. When a social caption is required, keep
hashtags minimal (≤3), topic-first, and never let them substitute for a
clear CTA in the body.

## Output structure (landing-page / sales-email mode)

```
[Headline — the promise in the customer's own words. Survives "so what?"]

[Sub — the proof / mechanism that makes the promise believable.]

[Open at the moment of highest tension — their situation today, concrete.]

[Mirror VOC language — they feel understood. ONE specific detail.]

[The mechanism — how it works, in plain language. ONE specific proof.]

[The single clear next step — name exactly what happens when they click.]
```

## Anti-patterns

```
✗ Inventing clever lines instead of mining the customer's own words
✗ Cleverness that costs clarity (a pun the reader has to decode)
✗ Vague benefit-speak — "amazing", "seamless", "world-class", "innovative"
✗ Feature-first openings ("Our platform offers...") instead of pain-first
✗ Selling at full intensity to an unaware reader
✗ Adjective stacking in place of a real, specific proof
✗ A vague CTA ("Get started", "Learn more") that hides the next step
✗ Over-voiced copy that performs personality instead of converting
```

## Example — universal (SaaS sign-up flow, brand-neutral)

### Headline options

```
1. "I stopped guessing where my users drop off."   (customer's own words)
2. See exactly which step loses your signups — in 3 minutes.   (outcome)
3. Your onboarding works. So why do 60% never come back?   (problem-aware)
```

### Landing-page section (full Wiebe structure)

```
"I stopped guessing where my users drop off."

A single session replay shows you the exact click they rage-quit on.

You ship the feature. The dashboard says signups are up. And then —
nothing. They never come back, and you're left reading charts that tell
you what happened but never why.

Watch one real session and you'll see it in ten seconds: the form field
that fails silently, the button no one finds, the step that asks for too
much too soon.

Set it up once. No code, no tag manager, no waiting on engineering —
paste one line and the next session records itself.

→ Start recording in 3 minutes. See your first drop-off today.
```

The difference from Halbert is the **anchor**: Halbert leads with a
deadline and the cost of inaction; Wiebe leads with the reader's *own
words* and the *moment of highest tension*, then earns the click with a
specific, demonstrable proof. Halbert pushes with urgency; Wiebe pulls
with recognition.

## Pairs with visual style

`editorial-documentary` (evidence-led, plain) — or `financial-restraint`
for a calmer SaaS/B2B register. Avoid `soft-maximalist`: ornament fights
this voice's clarity-first discipline.

## When NOT to use this voice

- Brand-prestige / heritage moments with no conversion goal (use
  `apple-jobsian` or `hermes-quiet-luxury`).
- Hard, deadline-driven offers where urgency is the lever (use
  `halbert-direct-response`).
- Anywhere you have **no** voice-of-customer input and can't get any —
  this voice is only as good as the customer language feeding it. Without
  research it degrades into generic SaaS copy.
