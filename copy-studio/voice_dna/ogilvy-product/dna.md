# Voice DNA — `ogilvy-product`

The Xalq Sigorta default for product-led campaigns. The voice that built
modern advertising's "headline = USP, body = evidence" tradition. The
consumer is never a moron, and she always gets the reason before the ask.

## Lineage

- **David Ogilvy**, "Confessions of an Advertising Man" (1963) and
  "Ogilvy on Advertising" (1983). The Rolls-Royce headline, the Pepperidge
  Farm tradition, the Hathaway shirt eyepatch.
- **Howard Gossage** — "Eagle Shirts" / "Sierra Club" — the long-copy
  Ogilvy lineage with more wit.
- **Bill Bernbach** for the rhythm; Ogilvy for the discipline.
- Recent practitioners: **Andy Maslen**, **Joanna Wiebe** (Copyhackers
  early work — before the conversion-optimization era took over).

## Voice fingerprint

- **Headline carries the product benefit.** If you removed the brand
  name and the headline still made sense as a generic statement, it's
  not Ogilvy. The headline must commit to ONE specific thing.
- **Body is reasoned, not emotional.** Evidence-based: numbers, specifics,
  quoted testimony. Earned trust, not declared.
- **Wit, never jokes.** A dry observation is Ogilvy. A pun is not.
- **No exclamation marks. Ever.** Even on triumphant copy.
- **Long-copy tradition.** Readers who want to know more will read more.
  Ogilvy never apologized for word count.
- **Specifics over generalizations.** "60 miles per hour" not "fast."
  "29 percent" not "many".

## Headline structure

The Ogilvy headline is one of three patterns:

1. **The Specific Promise**
   - "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this Rolls-Royce comes
      from the electric clock."
   - Format: [specific condition] + [specific outcome] + [product]

2. **The Restrained News**
   - "Pepperidge Farm Remembers."
   - Format: [brand] + [verb that asserts heritage]

3. **The Reason-To-Care**
   - "How to Avoid Holes in Your Socks." (Hathaway-era technique)
   - Format: How to [verb] [problem the reader has]

Length: 4-9 words. Period as punctuation, never exclamation.

## Sub-headline structure

The sub-headline does ONE of:
- Explains a single specific from the headline ("The electric clock
  alone takes 137 hours to assemble.")
- Sets the reader's expectation for body length ("Here is what makes
  this Rolls-Royce the best car in the world.")
- Soft CTA at the end of body, never at the start.

## Body copy structure

- **Open with the customer's existing situation**, not the brand.
- **One claim per paragraph**, each followed by evidence (number,
  quote, comparison, demonstration).
- **Read aloud.** Sentences average 12-18 words but vary widely.
  Short sentences punctuate. Long sentences accumulate.
- **No "We at Xalq Sigorta..."** — the brand appears in body only when
  evidence requires it.

## Punctuation discipline

- Periods. Commas. Em-dashes used sparingly.
- Semicolons allowed for compound thoughts.
- NO exclamation marks.
- NO ellipses (they signal hesitation; Ogilvy never hesitates).
- Sentence-case throughout. ALL CAPS reserved for the brand mark only.

## Hashtag posture (when used)

- 3-4 maximum. Brand + product category + topical anchor.
- Format: PascalCase (#XalqSigorta), not lowercase (#xalqsigorta).
- No emoji hashtags. No #blessed, no #vibes, no hashtag jokes.

## Caption structure (Instagram / LinkedIn)

Standard Ogilvy caption is:

```
[Headline — declarative, period.]

[Sub-headline — 1-2 sentences, expands the headline with a specific.]

[Body paragraph 1 — the customer's situation, one specific.]

[Body paragraph 2 — the product fact, one specific.]

[Soft CTA — phone + URL + branches. No urgency, no FOMO.]

#XalqSigorta · #ProductTag · #TopicalTag
```

## Reference phrases (fold into prompt instructions)

```
- "Ogilvy product-led headline tradition: headline carries the USP,
   body provides evidence, wit is dry not joking."
- "Specifics over generalizations. Numbers, quotes, comparisons."
- "No exclamation marks ever. Period as final punctuation."
- "Open the body with the customer's situation, not the brand."
- "Length is not the enemy. Boring is the enemy."
```

## Anti-patterns (what this voice is NOT)

```
✗ "Discover the new ..."  / "Imagine if ..."  — rhetorical openers
✗ "We're excited / thrilled / proud to ..." — corporate filler
✗ "Don't miss out!" / "Limited time only!" — direct-response urgency
✗ "Game-changing" / "Best-in-class" / "Next-level" — startup speak
✗ "Hassle-free" / "Peace of mind" — financial-services clichés
✗ Exclamation marks of any kind
✗ Emoji in body or headline
✗ Adjective stacking ("modern, fast, reliable")
✗ Rhetorical questions in headlines
```

## Example — Xalq Sigorta travel insurance, Ogilvy voice

### Headline (4 options, all valid)

```
1. Yeni reys başladı. Sığortanı al, yola çıx.
2. Bakı–Tbilisi qatarında bir sənəd unudulur. Bu, sənin sığortandır.
3. Gürcüstana giriş üçün lazım olan bir kağız.
4. 31 dekabra qədər sığortanı al. 1 yanvardan giriş üçün lazım olur.
```

### Caption (full Ogilvy structure, AZ)

```
Yeni reys başladı. Sığortanı al, yola çıx.

1 yanvar 2026-dan etibarən Gürcüstana giriş üçün
səyahət sığortası məcburidir.

Bakı–Tbilisi qatarı saat 21:40-da yola düşür və səhər
Tbilisidə olur. Səyahət iki gün çəkmir; sənədlərin də
hazırlanması iki günə qalmasın.

Xalq Sigorta səyahət sığortasını üç dəqiqədə hazırlayır.
Onlayn — bütün lazımi sənədlərlə birlikdə.

Hotline: 183
Onlayn: xalqsigorta.az

#XalqSigorta · #BakıTbilisi · #SəyahətSığortası
```

The difference from amateur copy:
- Headline is one specific commitment, not a slogan.
- Sub gives one number (the train schedule fact).
- Body opens with the customer's situation (the trip), then the
  product fact (3 minutes), then the soft CTA. Each paragraph one idea.
- No exclamation marks. No emoji. Three hashtags.
