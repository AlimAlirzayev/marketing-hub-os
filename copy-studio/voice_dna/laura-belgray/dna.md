# Voice DNA — `laura-belgray`

**Personality-driven email.** The voice for newsletters and list emails
that read like a funny friend writing to you — story-first, honest to the
point of oversharing, entertainment as the retention strategy. Her small
list out-converts lists 50x the size because subscribers actually *open*
her. Use when the brand can afford real personality and the goal is a
list that reads every send. Brand-agnostic (calibrate the humor to the
brand's tolerance).

## Lineage

- **Laura Belgray**, Talking Shrimp; *Tough Titties* (bestseller);
  co-creator of The Copy Cure (with Marie Forleo).
- 20+ years writing TV promos (Nickelodeon era) — hooks and premium
  entertainment compression.
- The story-first email tradition: the sale rides on the story, never
  the other way around.

## Voice fingerprint

- **Be yourself, amplified.** Copy as "100% expression of your sparkling
  personality." The reader should recognize the sender in one paragraph
  without a signature.
- **Story first, lesson second, offer last.** Every email opens with a
  real, specific, often self-deprecating story; the pivot to the point
  comes late and feels earned.
- **Painfully honest.** Overshare calibrated for charm — the embarrassing
  detail is the hook. Vulnerability builds the parasocial bond that
  converts.
- **Funny in the details.** The humor comes from specificity (the exact
  brand, the exact quote, the exact awkward moment), not from jokes.
- **Conversational and breezy.** Contractions, asides, fragments —
  written the way she talks.
- **Entertainment IS the value.** Tips are everywhere; a sender you
  *want* to open is rare. People gravitate to stories, not actionables.

## Subject-line structure

1. **The mid-story tease** — "The waiter said WHAT?"
2. **The confession** — "I got kicked out of a spin class."
3. **The curiosity fragment** — "this is embarrassing"

Lowercase, casual, personal — reads like a friend, never a brand blast.

## Body structure

- **Cold-open into the story.** No preamble, no "hope you're well."
  Scene first: where, when, the exact detail.
- **Ride the story** — dialogue, asides, the humiliating turn.
- **The pivot** — "Which is exactly like what happens with your copy…"
  The lesson lands because the story already did.
- **The offer, casually** — one link, one line, no hard sell posture.
- **P.S. does real work** — the second joke or the second link.

## Punctuation discipline

- **Dashes, ellipses, italics** — speech rhythm on the page.
- **Fragments** everywhere. Written like talk.
- **ALL CAPS** for the single emphasized word (sparingly).
- **Exclamation marks** — allowed (rare in our other voices), but earned,
  not decorative.

## Hashtag posture

None — this is email.

## Output structure (list email)

```
Subject: [mid-story tease / confession — lowercase, personal]

[Cold-open into the scene — the exact, specific, embarrassing detail.]

[The story rides — dialogue, asides, the turn.]

[The pivot — "which is exactly what happens when..."]

[The offer — one casual line, one link.]

P.S. [second joke or second chance at the link.]
```

## Reference phrases

```
- "Copy as 100% expression of your sparkling personality."
- "Story first, lesson second, offer last."
- "The humor is in the exact detail, not the joke."
- "Be a sender they want to open — entertainment is the value."
- "Written the way you talk, embarrassing bits included."
```

## Anti-patterns

```
✗ Corporate polish ("We're excited to announce...")
✗ Tips-first emails with no story
✗ Humor without specificity (generic jokes)
✗ Hiding the sender's personality behind the brand
✗ Hard-sell pivots that betray the story's trust
✗ "Hope this email finds you well" openings
```

## Example — universal (list email, brand-neutral)

```
Subject: I argued with a smoothie guy

So yesterday I ordered a smoothie and the guy asked, "Small, medium, or
Grande Wellness?"

Grande. Wellness.

I asked what's in it. He said — I quote — "basically vibes and spinach."
I paid $14 for vibes and spinach.

Here's the thing: I *bought* it. Because "Grande Wellness" made me feel
like a person with her life together, and "large spinach drink" does not.

That's what naming does. Nobody buys the ingredients. They buy who they
get to be while holding the cup.

If your product's still called the ingredients, I fix that — [link].

P.S. The smoothie was fine. The self-image was excellent.
```

## Pairs with visual style

`soft-maximalist` — playful, personal. Avoid `financial-restraint`; this
voice dies in a sterile frame.

## When NOT to use this voice

- Regulated, formal, or crisis comms (use `financial-restraint-az` /
  `ogilvy-product`).
- Structured retention flows (use `chase-dimond`).
- Brands that can't tolerate real personality — don't water it down,
  switch voices.
