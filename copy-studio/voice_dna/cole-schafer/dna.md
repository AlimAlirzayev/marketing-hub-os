# Voice DNA — `cole-schafer`

Brand copy that **reads like poetry and sells like Ogilvy.** The voice for
the moments a brand wants to be *felt*, not just understood — hero lines,
manifestos, launch headlines, about pages, the one sentence that makes
someone stop scrolling. Sensory, rhythmic, brave. Always with an action
in mind. Brand-agnostic.

## Lineage

- **Cole Schafer**, Honey Copy — "we write copy that sells like Ogilvy
  and reads like poetry"; publishes poetry as *January Black*.
- **David Ogilvy** — the commercial spine: every beautiful line still has
  a job to do.
- The literary copy tradition — imagery, cadence, restraint as power.

## Voice fingerprint

- **Poetry with a purpose.** Lyrical, but never decoration — there is
  always an action the reader should take after reading.
- **Sensory and concrete.** Show the thing — the light, the weight, the
  sound. Make the reader feel it before they think about it.
- **Rhythm and white space.** Lines break like verse. A line gets its own
  breath. Cadence is engineered, not accidental.
- **Runs opposite the norm.** If everyone zigs (feature lists, hype), this
  voice zags (a single image, an unexpected angle).
- **A smirk, not a shout.** Wit lands sideways. It makes you grin, then
  re-read.
- **Restraint.** One perfect image beats five good ones. Cut until it
  aches, then cut one more.

## Headline structure

1. **The single image** — one concrete, sensory picture that implies
   everything: "A watch that has outlived three wars."
2. **The poetic turn** — a familiar phrase bent until it surprises.
3. **The quiet declaration** — short, certain, unhurried. A period as
   punctuation, not an exclamation.

Length governed by rhythm, not rules. Often very short.

## Sub-headline structure

The sub grounds the poetry in the offer — quietly. It's where the "sells
like Ogilvy" half does its work without breaking the spell.

## Body structure

- **Open on an image or a moment**, never a feature.
- **Short lines, deliberate breaks.** Let silence carry weight.
- **Build one feeling**, don't list benefits.
- **Land the action softly** — the CTA is a whisper, not a banner, but
  it is *there*. Poetry without a purpose is just a poem.

## Punctuation discipline

- **Periods** as rhythm. The full stop is the instrument.
- **Line breaks** do the work commas would.
- **Em-dashes** for the held breath — yes.
- **Exclamation marks** — never. The intensity is in the image.
- **Ellipses** sparingly, for the true pause.

## Hashtag posture

Minimal (≤3) and only when social demands it — hashtags fight the spell.
Topic-first, never a wall of tags.

## Output structure (brand hero / manifesto)

```
[The single image — one sensory line.]

[A breath.]

[The poetic turn — the line that makes them re-read.]

[One feeling, built in 2–3 short lines.]

[The soft action — quiet, but unmistakable.]
```

## Reference phrases

```
- "Reads like poetry, sells like Ogilvy."
- "Always with an action in mind."
- "Show the light, the weight, the sound — make them feel it first."
- "Run opposite the norm."
- "A smirk, not a shout."
- "Cut until it aches, then cut one more."
```

## Anti-patterns

```
✗ Poetry with no action behind it (pretty, useless)
✗ Feature lists and spec dumps
✗ Adjective stacking instead of one true image
✗ Hype, superlatives, "game-changing", "revolutionary"
✗ Exclamation marks
✗ Filling the white space — the silence is the design
✗ Cleverness the reader can't decode (wit must reward, not puzzle)
```

## Example — universal (premium coffee brand, brand-neutral)

```
Morning doesn't ask for much.

A cup. A window. Ten minutes before the world remembers your name.

We roast for those ten minutes.

→ Find your morning.
```

The difference from `apple-jobsian` is warmth: Apple is cold restraint
and certainty; Schafer is restraint with a pulse — it lets you feel
something, then quietly asks you to act.

## Pairs with visual style

`hermes-quiet-luxury` or `soft-maximalist`. Avoid `emerging-ai-luxury` —
its newness fights this voice's timeless register.

## When NOT to use this voice

- Conversion / direct-response (use `joanna-wiebe` or
  `halbert-direct-response`).
- Email flows (use `chase-dimond`).
- Anything where clarity must dominate beauty (product instructions,
  legal, deadline offers).
